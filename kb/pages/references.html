References

The intellectual lineage behind this knowledge base — key thinkers, texts, and traditions that inform the Gesture + Lineage approach.

Thinkers

Game Studies & Design

Ian Bogost — Persuasive Games, How to Do Things with Videogames. Procedural rhetoric: videogames make arguments through rules. Foundational for Meaning From Dynamics. Brendan Keogh — A Play of Bodies. Phenomenological approach to videogames. The body as site of meaning. Influences my emphasis on embodied experience. Kristine Jørgensen — Gameworld Interfaces. UI as part of the fiction. Influences Feedback Readability. Steve Swink — Game Feel. The definitive text on real-time control feel. Essential for understanding Gesture. Anna Anthropy — Rise of the Videogame Zinesters, Game Design Vocabulary. Player-centric vocabulary, accessible design language. Jesse Schell — The Art of Videogame Design. The lenses framework. See The Design Lens. Jesper Juul — Half-Real. Rules and fiction in games. Foundational videogame studies text. Janet Murray — Hamlet on the Holodeck. Early digital media theory, still relevant for procedurality and agency.

Design Theory

Donald Schön — The Reflective Practitioner. Design as conversation with materials. Core to Code As Material. Christopher Alexander — A Pattern Language. Architecture as communication. Influences level design thinking. Don Norman — The Design of Everyday Things. Affordances, feedback in physical design. Applies directly to videogame interfaces.

Psychology & Learning

Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi — Flow. The optimal experience that good pacing supports. See Player Psychology. John Dewey — Experience and Education. Learning by doing. Philosophical backbone of Play Name Make Reflect. Seymour Papert — Mindstorms. Coding as thinking tool. Influences Debugging As Literacy.

Classic Videogame Studies

Johan Huizinga — Homo Ludens. The "magic circle." Influential but contested. See What Makes A Game. Roger Caillois — Man, Play and Games. Taxonomy of play (agon, alea, mimicry, ilinx). Bernard Suits — The Grasshopper. "Games as voluntary attempt to overcome unnecessary obstacles."

Traditions

Phenomenology

The philosophical tradition focused on lived experience. Key idea: meaning is bodied, not just mental. Videogames are experienced through hands and bodies, not just interpreted by minds. Key figures: Merleau-Ponty, Husserl Game studies application: Keogh's work on embodiment

Pragmatism

American philosophical tradition. Key idea: truth is what works for a purpose. Design knowledge is validated through making, not just theorizing. Key figures: Dewey, James, Peirce Game studies application: Design as practical knowledge

Systems Theory

The study of complex systems and their behaviors. Key idea: systems have emergent properties not predictable from parts. Key figures: Meadows, Forrester Game studies application: Feedback Loops, Emergent Behavior

Studio Pedagogy

Teaching through making and critique, from art and architecture schools. Key idea: you learn by doing and reflecting. Application: Play Name Make Reflect

What I Take, What I Leave

This knowledge base doesn't adopt any theory wholesale. For each thinker/tradition: From Bogost: Procedural rhetoric, games-as-arguments. Leaving: Sometimes too formalist. From Keogh: Embodiment, phenomenology. Leaving: Can be inaccessible writing. From Swink: Feel vocabulary, real-time control. Leaving: Narrow focus on action games. From Schell: Lenses, comprehensive view. Leaving: Can be encyclopedic without depth. From Huizinga: Magic circle concept. Leaving: The hard separation between videogame and life is false. From videogame studies generally: Rigorous analysis. Leaving: Often divorced from making. This is a maker's knowledge base, not a scholar's. Theory serves practice.
