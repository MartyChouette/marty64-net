Dialogue Systems

A dialogue system is the interface and logic that governs conversation in a game. It determines how players talk to characters, how characters respond, and how conversations flow. Dialogue systems are where narrative meets interface design. The structure of conversation shapes what kinds of stories you can tell.

Core Components

Every dialogue system has:

Triggering

How do conversations start? - Automatic (enter area, story triggers) - Player-initiated (talk button near NPC) - Contextual (interrupt after certain actions)

Display

How is dialogue presented? - Text boxes - Voiced audio - Subtitles - Speech bubbles - Full-screen conversational UI

Player Input

How does the player participate? - Advance text (passive) - Select from options - Type responses - Timed choices - No input (cinematic)

Flow Control

How does conversation progress? - Linear (one path) - Branching (multiple paths) - Hub-and-spoke (return to menu of topics) - State-based (available options depend on flags)

Common Structures

Simple Linear

Player advances through fixed text. No choices. 
NPC: "The bridge is out."
NPC: "You'll have to find another way."
[End]

Use for: exposition, atmospheric flavor, world-building

Menu Selection

Player chooses from a list of topics/responses. 
> Ask about the bridge
> Ask about the village
> Say goodbye

Use for: information gathering, controlled exploration

Branching Tree

Choices lead to different dialogue paths that may reconverge or diverge. 
> "I'll help you" ‚Üí [Grateful response] ‚Üí ...
> "Not my problem" ‚Üí [Annoyed response] ‚Üí ...

Use for: roleplaying, consequence, character expression

Hub and Spoke

Keep returning to a central menu after each topic is explored.

The Option Problem

What the player sees vs. what the character says is a design choice.

Mass Effect famously used a tone-based wheel that sometimes delivered lines players didn't expect. Disco Elysium shows full text. Neither is "correct" ‚Äî they serve different experiences.

Conversation as Gameplay

Dialogue can be more than information delivery ‚Äî it can be play. Examples: Ace Attorney: Present evidence during testimony, catch contradictions Disco Elysium: Skill checks embedded in dialogue, your thoughts argue with each other Hades: Brief, contextual exchanges that deepen over dozens of runs When dialogue is gameplay, it needs: - Stakes (what can I win or lose?) - Player agency (what decisions matter?) - Feedback (how do I know what worked?)

Teaching Notes

Dialogue systems are where writing meets UI/UX. I teach this in the Narrative Design section but emphasize the systems aspect. Exercise: Dialogue Autopsy Take a dialogue sequence from a game. Map: 1. What's the structure? (Linear, branching, hub?) 2. How is text displayed? 3. What player input is required? 4. How are options presented? Exercise: Conversation as Game Design a dialogue encounter where conversation IS the gameplay ‚Äî there's a win/lose state, player decisions matter, and there's feedback.

Theoretical Background

Key influences: Infocom ‚Äî Parser-based interactive fiction that pioneered computer conversation. BioWare ‚Äî The dialogue wheel, romance options, companion relationships. ZA/UM (Disco Elysium) ‚Äî Pushed dialogue as gameplay. Your skills are characters in the conversation. What I'm taking: Dialogue is interface. The structure of conversation enables or constrains what stories can be told. Unresolved questions: - How will LLMs change dialogue systems? Potentially infinite variation, but loss of authorial control. - Is there a "natural" dialogue pacing?
