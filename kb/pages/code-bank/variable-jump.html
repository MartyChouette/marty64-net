Variable Jump

A jump where height depends on how long you hold the button. Tap for a short hop, hold for a full jump. This is the standard for modern platformers — it gives players expressive control over their arc.

The Feel

Variable jump creates a sense of responsive precision. The player feels like the character is listening to their intention, not just executing a canned animation. Compare: - Fixed jump: "I pressed jump and it happened" - Variable jump: "I shaped that jump" The difference is ownership.

Exposed Variables

VariableTypeDefaultWhat it does

minJumpForcefloat8.0Jump height if you tap (minimum) maxJumpForcefloat16.0Jump height if you hold (maximum) holdTimeForMaxfloat0.2Seconds of hold to reach max height gravityfloat40.0Downward acceleration fallGravityMultiplierfloat1.5Extra gravity when falling (for snappier descent) jumpCutMultiplierfloat0.5How much to reduce velocity when you release early

Tuning Guide

For a floaty, forgiving feel:

- Lower gravity (25-30) - Lower fallGravityMultiplier (1.0-1.2) - Longer holdTimeForMax (0.3-0.4) - Higher maxJumpForce Reference: Kirby, Little Big Planet

For a tight, precise feel:

- Higher gravity (45-60) - Higher fallGravityMultiplier (1.8-2.5) - Shorter holdTimeForMax (0.1-0.15) - More dramatic ratio between min and max force Reference: Celeste, Super Meat Boy

For a weighty, committed feel:

- Smaller gap between minJumpForce and maxJumpForce - Lower jumpCutMultiplier (0.7-0.8) so releasing early doesn't change much - Higher gravity Reference: Dark Souls, Castlevania

How It Works

On Jump Press:
  - Apply minJumpForce upward
  - Start tracking hold time
While Holding (and still rising):
  - Interpolate toward maxJumpForce based on hold time
On Jump Release (while rising):
  - Multiply current upward velocity by jumpCutMultiplier
  - Stop adding force
While Falling:
  - Apply gravity * fallGravityMultiplier

The key insight: you're not choosing jump height at the start. You're shaping the arc in real-time by continuing or releasing the button.

Common Issues

"The jump feels mushy" → Increase fallGravityMultiplier. The descent should be snappier than the ascent. "I can't do small hops" → Lower minJumpForce or increase jumpCutMultiplier effect. "The max jump takes too long to reach" → Shorten holdTimeForMax. Most players hold for 0.1-0.2 seconds max. "It feels like two different jumps instead of a range" → The interpolation might be too binary. Check that you're smoothly blending, not just switching at a threshold.

Combine With

- Coyote Time — grace period makes variable jump more forgiving - Input Buffer — catch jump presses just before landing - Squash Stretch — visual feedback that matches the jump's force - Air Control — what you can do while in the arc you shaped

Heritage Notes

Variable jump emerged in the early 1980s as designers realized fixed jumps felt unresponsive. Super Mario Bros. (1985) is the canonical example, but the technique appeared earlier in various forms. The problem it solved: fixed jumps force level design to accommodate the jump, not the player. With variable jump, the player adapts to the level. This enables tighter, more demanding platforming. Modern platformers have refined the technique with additions like: - Jump buffering (input forgiveness) - Coyote time (spatial forgiveness) - Fall gravity multiplier (making descent feel distinct from ascent) When you tune a variable jump, you're participating in forty years of accumulated craft knowledge about how jumps should feel.
